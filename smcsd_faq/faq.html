3. What is a charter school?

An independent public charter school in California is a public school which is funded by local and state taxes but is run as an independent organization usually with its own board. Local school boards have limited control over charter schools other than the authorization process. Willow Creek Academy is considered an 'independent' charter. It's charter is authorized by the Sausalito Marin City School District, which provides oversight of the school.

7. What is meant by student 'growth'? and what do educators think is typical or reasonable to expect in terms of student growth in standardized text scores year to year?

Student-growth measures compare the relative change in a student or group of students' performance on a specific test with the performance of all other students on that same test. The scores of all students are used to create an “index of student growth” and to identify a median achievement score that can be used as a point of comparison for all student scores—i.e., some students will show growth that is greater than the median, while others will show growth that is lower than the median. There is no standard for student growth in test scores year-to-year. However, many states that administered the same standardized test with the same questions—known as the Smarter Balanced Assessment—saw no significant change in student growth in 2017 testing as compared to the year before.

10. What is FCMAT?

FCMAT is the Financial Crisis & Financial Assistance Team. It is a state organization created to provide fiscal and management advice and technical support to local school districts. FCMAT has been requested to review district operations at least three times, in 1997, 2012 and 2016.

11. What can cause the county or state to take over a school district?

Generally speaking a county or state can can take control of a local school district, or Local Education Agency, if it is determined that the district is fiscally insolvent and unable to meet its financial obligations. In this case, the state will issue a loan and maintain control of the district until certain conditions are met.

16. What is Local Control Funding Formula?

The Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) determines how much funding school districts recieve from the state. The formula replaces a system based on historic revenue limits with a per student calculation. The new formula was introduced in the 2013-14 school year, with full implementation of the new funding formula expected to take eight years. Under the new funding formula, school districts are subject to new rules for transparency and accountability, which include creating—with input from parents and the community—and adopting a Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP) that lays out that lays out how the LEA will spend the funds and its goals for improving student outcomes.

17. What is a Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP)?

LCAP is the Local Control and Accountability Plan. It is a three year plan updated yearly that includes specific goals, actions, and metrics for the school district. School budget allocations are tied to LCAP goals for improving student outcomes according to eight priorities that the legislature set. The priorities include academic achievement, student engagement, parent involvement, and the successful implementation of new academic standards. Districts that fail to meet their goals and improve student outcomes will receive help through a new agency, the California Collaborative for Educational Excellence. Both the school district and Willow Creek Academy (as a separate LEA) must develop and maintain an LCAP for their respective schools.

18. What is the difference between a 'basic aid' district and a 'revenue limit' district?

A 'basic aid' district is one whose local property taxes meet or exceed the state-manded per student funding formula, known as LCFF, that determines state funding to local school districts. A 'basic aid' district meets its school funding requirements entirely through property taxes and receive no general purpose state aid. It also retains any excess property taxes within its district. By contrast, in 'revenue limit' districts, also known as 'LCFF' districts (which stands for Locally Control Funded Formula), property taxes do not exceed the state mandated per student funding calculation; therefore the state must make up the difference. Only the state can determine revenue-limit or basic aid status through the revenue limit formula. In Marin County, only four districts are revenue-limit districts: Novato, San Rafael elementary only, Ross Valley and Mill Valley are revenue limit. Mill Valley changed from being a 'basic-aid' funded district to being a 'revenue limit' district in 2013-2014 when the state provided per student funding exceeded property tax revenues.The remaining 16 are basic aid and receive their funds directly from their local property taxes.

19. What is meant by "basic aid excess / negative excess"?

Basic aid excess' refers to the amount of funding that exceeds the funding the district would have received were it considered a revenue limit district. It is calculated by subtracting the state mandated Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) revenue amounts from the total revenue received by the district. In years when it's budgeted expenses (including LCFF mandated expenses as well as special education, facilities costs, capital expenses, district administration and overhead costs) exceed total revenue, creating a negative balance, the district has what is referred to as a 'negative basic aid excess.' Put simply, its basic aid revenues are not sufficient to cover its shared expenses and LCFF entitlements. Whether or not or how expenses such as special education costs and facilities costs should be shared between the traditional school and the charter school impacts the basic aid excess/negative excess calculation is an issue raised by FCMAT in its reports.

20. How is the school tax rate determined?

When the ballot measure Proposition 13 was approved by voters in 1978 it capped property taxes limiting the revenues available to counties, cities, and special districts to provide services. Subsequent legislation froze the proportions of property tax allocations among local governments to their pre–Proposition 13 levels. Today, approximately 16% of each tax dollar that residents in the City of Sausalito pay is distributed to the elementary school district according to the Marin County Department of Finance. By comparison, Mill Valley distributes 18% of tax revenues to its elementary school district. Tiburon distributes 17.5% and San Rafael distributes 22% of tax revenues to its elementary school district. Comparable information was not available for the unincorporated community of Marin City.

22. What is a 'parcel tax'

A parcel tax is a tax levied on properties in addition to property tax. Unlike a property tax, it is a flat-tax as opposed to a tax based on the assessed value of the property. In California, it is often used to provide additional funding to schools. The Sausalito Marin City School District does not currently receive parcel tax funding. You can view a list of which districts in Marin County receive funding from parcel taxes on the County of Marin's Office of Education website.

23. What is a bond and when was the last time the district recieved bond funding?

Bond funds are a debt instrument and are typically used to fund capital improvements such as the construction or renovation of facilities. The last time the district received bond funding was in 2006 after voters approved Measure I in 2004 in the amount of $15.9 million for facility construction and upgrades. In August 2016, after conducting a facilities review and the school board approved putting a $30 million dollar bond measure before voters. However, the district missed the filing deadline for the November election, and the measure was not included on the ballot. In recent board meetings, the school board has stated it's intention to develop a facilities plan before moving ahead with a bond measure.
